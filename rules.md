# Phonics Core Rules (1–9)

This document defines the core phonics rules in a recommended learning and organization order.  
Each rule is atomic and can be expanded into its own standalone webpage.

---

## 1. Short Vowels

The five vowels **a, e, i, o, u** usually make **short vowel sounds** in closed syllables.

**Typical pattern**
- CVC (consonant–vowel–consonant)

**Examples**
- cat, bed, sit, hot, cup

---

## 2. Hard and Soft Consonants

Some consonants change their sound depending on the following vowel.

**Rules**
- **c**
  - /k/ → cat, cup
  - /s/ → city, cent
- **g**
  - /g/ → go, game
  - /dʒ/ → giant, gem

---

## 3. Digraphs

A **digraph** is two letters that work together to make **one single sound**.

**Common digraphs**
- sh, ch, th, wh, ph

**Examples**
- ship, chair, this, what, phone

---

## 4. Consonant Blends

A **blend** is two or more consonants together where **each sound is heard**.

**Common blends**
- bl, st, tr, sm

**Examples**
- black, stop, tree, smile

---

## 5. Magic E (Silent E)

A final **e** at the end of a word is silent but makes the vowel say its **long sound**.

**Pattern**
- CVCe

**Examples**
- cap → cape  
- hop → hope  
- kit → kite

---

## 6. Vowel Teams

A **vowel team** is two vowels together that work as a team to make a sound.  
Often, the first vowel is long and the second is silent (not always).

**Common vowel teams**
- ai, ee, ea, oa, ay

**Examples**
- rain, see, eat, boat, play

---

## 7. R-Controlled Vowels

When a vowel is followed by **r**, the vowel sound changes.  
Standard short and long vowel rules do not apply.

**Common patterns**
- ar, er, ir, or, ur

**Examples**
- car, her, bird, fork, turn

---

## 8. Open and Closed Syllables

Syllable type affects vowel sounds.

**Closed syllable**
- Ends with a consonant  
- Vowel is short  
- Example: cat

**Open syllable**
- Ends with a vowel  
- Vowel is long  
- Example: me

---

## 9. Double Consonants and Spelling Patterns

Some spelling patterns help signal pronunciation or spelling rules.

**Common patterns**
- **ck** follows a short vowel (back)
- **ff, ll, ss** often appear at the end of words (bell, miss)
- **tch** usually follows a short vowel (match)
